Public Statement

Our overall aim is to develop new data science methods that help to improve the process of conducting and reporting research, and to use these developments to improve our national capability in genomics and population health and ecology research, supporting our bio-economy and at the same time improving health and environmental outcomes.

The team has achieved significant research progress this year in our three distinct areas:

  1. bioinformatics—studying the relationships between genomics and disease to help improve our response to agricultural disease outbreaks and human health outcomes;

  2. ecological modelling—creating new models to study the entire genome of threatened and invasive species to assess ecological impact and remediation;

  3. live and transparent data science—creating new ways of publishing and updating science studies to keep them up to date as new data and methods emerge, reducing the time that researchers spend updating, re-running and reporting their findings.  In each of these areas we have developed new methods and related software that has been shared openly with the science community.

Collaborations within our team are now leading to additional grant proposals in the areas of AI and genomics, and we have strengthened our collaborations with other research partners based in Aotearoa: notably Public Health Science (formerly EHR), Genomics Aotearoa, the RNA Platform, DoC, MPI, MoH and overseas: The Allen AI institute and Argonne National Labs - USA, Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR) and the Robert Koch Institute - Germany, Institute of Zoology - UK, and ETH -Switzerland).

This year our outreach & training event—ResBaz 2025 —provided hands-on data science training to over 2300 individual researchers throughout the country, that they can apply in their own research.  We are also training a future generation of NZ data scientists via our summer placements, PhD scholarships and post-doctoral appointments.  

Strategic Intent

We have placed more emphasis on AI methods in science over the last year. These featured previously, but now of course the number of opportunities in. this area is increasing rapidly. We are now using generative AI in our work on Transparent & Trusted research, in our project that dynamically updates research reports and in our bioinformatics research.

Our strategic direction focuses on developing new data science and AI methods that improve the conduct of research, and to showcase some of these improvements in action, in our specialist areas of: Biology, Genomics, Population Health and Ecology.  We provide a high-level summary of our work towards these strategic directions below.

Our research on trustworthy, explainable artificial intelligence aligns directly with MBIE’s goal of enabling a digital economy built on trust and transparency. We have begun a new project focused on hallucination detection and mechanistic interpretability, research that helps us move past treating artificial intelligence models as “black boxes” to uncover how and why they arrive at their outputs. By identifying the internal circuits and representations that drive their behaviour, we can better improve and audit them in a scientifically rigorous way. For New Zealand, developing innovation and expertise in this field is particularly important: it ensures that we are not just adopters of overseas AI technology, but leaders in shaping it and applying it responsibly.

Our Live Data Science initiative has now dramatically closed the gap between the ‘doing’ of science and the ‘publishing’ of science.  We have now developed workflows that update ‘live’ research documents in real time, in response to external triggers such as new data, better algorithms, or further experimentation. In an era when data are ubiquitous and regularly updated, there need no longer be a lag between writing and publishing science—whether in academic journals or in reports for business or government.  Furthermore, since this process is now automated and supported by generative AI, the human effort required to update reports has been significantly reduced, saving time for researchers and enabling them to be more productive. This work supports New Zealand’s broader strategy in AI for education and research and contributes to international discussions on trustworthy AI and responsible innovation.

Our Transparent Data Science improves the trustworthiness and reproducibility of data-intensive research, providing tools to question scientific claims and query research processes.  This opens up research to more scrutiny, which helps to improve confidence, that claims made about research hold water and are not exaggerated. and offers new avenues for public engagement. The tool we have built here is currently being evaluated for commercialisation and a limited rollout for evaluation. It is unique, in that it requires no training, and it reports not only its inner reasoning but also its confidence in its findings.

Our work applying data science methods in the Life Sciences tackles a multitude of longstanding analytical challenges, including: (i) new statistical techniques to improve the accuracy of phylodynamic modelling, with applications into population health and infectious diseases, (ii) modelling the entire genome of a species, not just an individual, and using this genome to anticipate adaptation capacity to a changing environment (iii) understanding the complex relationships between population genomics and common illnesses, to better target health interventions and (iv) better understanding and thus ability to control the spread of infectious diseases through susceptible populations. Some particular highlights for the past year include:

  • Developing Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) methods for parametric coalescent models in population genetics, advancing statistical techniques for dealing with model uncertainty in biological inference. This work contributes to more reliable understanding of population dynamics and demographic history reconstruction using genetic data.

  • Working on joint inference of genotype variants and cell lineage phylogeny and developing the calibrated coalescent point process - both advancing computational methods for complex biological data analysis.

  • Developing regularised conditional clade distributions to enhance phylogenetic posterior estimation, improving the accuracy and reliability of evolutionary inference.

  • Building phylogenetic inference methods through work on detecting rogue taxa and credible set estimation.

We have also started a new workplan that buildis individual-based genetic models that can assess and predict the ability of invasive species to adapt and expand their range, which has significant implications for understanding the likelihood of new biosecurity incursions, the further spread of already resident invasive species, and their subsequent economic and ecological impact.

Progress such as this not only moves forward our understanding of genomics and phylogenetics, but also provides better tools to our practicing bioinformatics researchers, working to support New Zealand’s bio-economy.

Impact

Our modelling biodiversity sub-theme has continued to develop reproducible, open source methods to measure genetic diversity and infer adaptability over time and space. Our research to understand the adaptive capacity of threatened species, using the hihi - stitchbird as a model system, has informed the development of management strategies for hihi and has also developed expertise in our team that is being utilised for species recovery more generally. For example Anna Santure was invited by the Department of Conservation to provide expert advice to genetic management strategies for our rarest bird, tara iti (NZ fairy tern), and was also invited onto a Genomics Aotearoa project to understand the genetic underpinnings of disease risk in hoiho (yellow eyed penguin), which have faced extreme population losses on mainland New Zealand while maintaining relatively healthy populations in the Subantarctic islands. This work makes a key contribution to understanding how DNA sequencing costs can be minimised while obtaining sufficient information to be able to conduct genomic analyses by 'imputing', (i.e., inferring) missing data.

Over the last two years we have been applying some of our new computational scaling methods to Bayesian phylogenetics. We have developed a new method for accurately estimating the distribution of phylogenetic trees of a phylogenetic analysis. This new method provides better probability estimates and more accurate summary trees compared to previous state-of-the-art techniques.  We have published these results and the methods are now implemented within the BEAST2 software (https://www.beast2.org/), now made available for other researchers and industry to use. Our work significantly improves a longstanding method, and we expect the new summary tree method to positively impact hundreds of phylogenetic research projects per year.

Within the last year our LivePublication project, a standards-based framework which connects experiment infrastructure with procedural dynamic research articles, has matured with the development of open-source software and a fully realised case study aimed at detecting changes in New Zealand’s tides, beaches and coastal reaches. This environmental exemplar aligns with national priorities in climate resilience and sustainable land use and has provided the first public deployment of LivePublication. With the framework now stable and domain-agnostic, we are ready to onboard additional high-impact case studies with partner groups.  All code used to develop and create LivePublication is open and freely reusable and reproducible.

The CoastSat LivePublication can be found here https://coastsat.livepublication.org/. The expertise gained on making the CoastSat LivePublication using the framework will allow us to help other researchers and projects to make use of this to more efficiently and regularly update time-critical reports and research investigations.

We have also implemented a provenance tracking system that annotates AI-generated versus human-written content. In addition, we developed a prototype GraphRAG database and DocSQL query engine with Kùzu DB, enabling AI to query research literature more accurately and transparently. These tools were shared in workshops and demos with positive researcher feedback, and our open-source releases on GitHub have begun attracting community engagement.

Kernel-Enriched AI (KEA) Explain is a neurosymbolic technique we have developed for detecting and explaining hallucinations in large language models. It provides not only improved accuracy in identifying errors but also explanations that help users understand why a response may be unreliable. The application of this method can lead to more trustworthy adoption of AI in sensitive domains such as government, education, and health, where factual reliability is critical.

We are working to understanding how knowledge is reorganised inside neural networks during the process of distillation, where smaller AI models are trained from larger ones. The impact of this work lies in improving the design and evaluation of smaller, more efficient AI systems. For New Zealand, this has particular significance: distilled models reduce computational and energy demands, making advanced AI systems viable for organisations with limited resources, including small businesses, government agencies, and researchers. Yet, little is known about how the internals of the models change structurally during the distillation process; our work is the first to do that. Developing expertise in this area positions New Zealand to build and deploy reliable AI that is not only powerful but also sustainable and cost-effective.

This work has been published (see excellence below) and shared as open source software: https://github.com/Reih02/hallucination_explanation_graph_kernel_analysis.

Our scientific claim extraction work has already been tested on large scientific corpora and provides a scalable pipeline for knowledge organization. The research supports New Zealand’s reputation in advancing AI for science, contributing to international efforts on research integrity and scientific discovery. Our open-source datasets and code for claim extraction/verification workflows are publicly shared via GitHub to encourage adoption.

Collaboration and Partnerships (Domestic and international collaboration)

Live and Transparent Data Science  We have built this year on our collaboration from Globus Labs (https://labs.globus.org/ and Argonne National Labs, Chicago, USA).  This collaboration is focussed on capturing workflow provenance and the generation of provenance artifacts seamlessly without the user’s intervention, thus allowing us to create a complete and reproducible representation of analytical activity.  Both partners are contributing time and technical resources to the project.  As an outcome of this collaboration Ellerm (PhD student) presented at the industry Conference “Globus World” in Chicago, USA, as the featured closing presentation. Throughout the year we have also continued to build ties with the RO-Crate community—which is distributed throughout the EU, and Australia.

Partnering with Stencila (a New Zealand software company) our work on AI content provenance and GraphRAG directly supports New Zealand’s national data science priorities of trustworthy AI, openness, reproducibility, and responsible digital infrastructure. By enabling researchers to clearly distinguish human- from AI-generated content, we address an important transparency issue that aligns with NZ government guidance on trustworthy AI use. The GraphRAG and DocSQL initiatives further contribute by making scientific literature queryable as structured data, enabling more reliable AI assistance and supporting policy directions around open science, innovation, and data-driven decision-making.

The project benefited from an Astera Institute Open Science Fellowship (Dec 2023–Mar 2025), which provided resources and mentoring to advance Stencila’s AI integration. This fellowship complemented MBIE support and underscored the project’s international relevance. We also established a collaboration with Kùzu DB, whose technical guidance and feature development accelerated our GraphRAG and DocSQL work. No EU Horizon or NZ industry co-funding was received during this period.

Domestically, Stencila partnered with Dragonfly Data Science, whose technical expertise helped accelerate AI feature development. Internationally, the Astera Fellowship created a strong link with the open science community, while collaboration with Kùzu DB (Canada) advanced our technical capacity in graph-based retrieval.

Trusted Data Science  We continue to partner with the Allen AI Institute (Based in Seattle), on a significant project called Nora. This extensive initiative involves multiple teams creating sub-agents designed to develop Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)-like algorithms. With our main collaborator, Niket Tandon, we are contributing to the scientific claim verification team, currently focusing on evaluating the claims in sets of research papers. This partnership is allowing us to access some of their state-of-the-art GenAI capabilities, experimental methods and compute infrastructure that is much larger and more performant than anything available currently in New Zealand. It also adds around 0.5 FTE to our project, funded by Allen AI.

Biological Data Sciences   Hui Zhen Tan and Eirian Perkins presented their research to the Hihi Recovery Group as part of a Hihi Research Evening in February 2024. This led to discussions on the translocation history of Hihi and, following a literature review of all previous Hihi. This research utilises information from whole genome resequencing data and a Hihi genome assembly, funded by Genomics Aotearoa. We continue to work closely with the Department of Conservation Hihi Recovery group and our results have supported the development of management strategies, including translocations between current hihi populations to bolster genetic diversity.

We have strong connections to the High Quality Genomics and Population Genomics projects within Genomics Aotearoa, which Anna Santure co-leads.  Additionally Co-PI Paul Gardner is interacting with RNA Platform pillar leads Dr. Sarah Diermeier & Dr. William Kelton to discuss bioinformatic method design challenges for the “target selection” and “payload design” challenges for RNA vaccines. We are collaborating with the RNA Platform to develop a pipeline for analysing pathogen genomes and identifying likely vaccine targets, in addition we are developing tools for optimising RNA sequences for vaccine applications.

For our population health focus, our main international collaborators on the salmonellosis project (Salmonella Enteritidis ST 183) are the German Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR) and the Robert Koch Institute (also in Germany), and the Institute of Zoology, Zoological Society of London. They have provided genomic data and expertise on salmonellosis in Europe.  The Neisseria meningitidis project, based in New Zealand represent important collaborations between ESR, the Ministry for Primary Industries, the Ministry of Health and the food industry along with Massey University, the University of Auckland and the University of Canterbury, linking expertise in data science, genomics, phylodynamics, epidemiology and public health.  

This work has major implications for how two major diseases of public health concern are controlled. They will benefit public health and policy makers, and grow capability in genomic epidemiology in ESR, providing career opportunities for two excellent researchers.
