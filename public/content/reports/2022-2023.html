Live Data Science—closing the gap between doing science and publishing science

The methods we use for sharing our science findings is woefully out of date and inefficient. In large part they have not changed in 350 years. Our aim here is to enable researchers to create an accurate, complete, verifiable and therefore trustworthy record of their research. Transparent Data Science—increasing trust in scientific claims

There is a crisis of public trust in science and in scientists. Alternative 'facts' can be damaging to public discourse if they are not grounded in any evidence, and if they cannot be easily differentiated from established truths. We urgently need tools that can test the validity of scientific claims, to enable policy discourse that is based on evidence and not hearsay.

Learning how to verify the claims in scientific papers, science press releases or even on social media is a difficult task for both artificial intelligence (AI) systems and humans. However, this task is crucial because

learning to separate verified facts from speculation or falsehoods has important consequences. Success at this task can help the reader understand scientific topics and promote science. Conversely, failure at this task leads to the spread of misinformation and exaggeration, which can cause distortion in scientific communication and undermine public confidence in science.

Training machine learning models to successfully perform scientific fact-checking tasks is has to this point been challenging due to the expertise bottleneck that limits the availability of appropriate training datasets. In this task, models use textual evidence to confirm scientific claims, which requires data that can be used to train the models—i.e. extensive domain-expert annotation. Consequently, the number of existing scientific-fact-checking datasets and the sizes of those datasets are limited. However, these limitations do not apply to multiple-choice question datasets because of the necessity of domain exams in the modern education system.

Our work over the past year has overcome this bottleneck, allowing us to assess the validity of science claims against all of the published scientific literature, without the need for targetting just one narrow domain of science, and without extensive training beforehand. This is a significant breakthrough, attained in part because of the successes of Large Language Models used in Deep Learning.

Over the past year, we've developed a scientific reading comprehension system, enhancing transparency and trustworthiness in scientific claims. This allows a user to test any science claim (e.g. Covid vaccines cause sterility in males) against the published science literature. Each claim (and its opposite) is given a compound score based on how much published science supports the claim, and the various ranking metrics used to evaluate science (such as journal standing and number of citations). The most pertinent research articles are summarised for the user in the light of their specific question, so they can more easily see why a particular argument might be true, or not.

We have submitted a research paper to a top NLP conference [1], presented at another prestigious NLP conference [2], and published our datasets, all in line with promoting Open Science principles. In the coming year, we'll focus on achieving excellence by refining our system using cutting-edge data science and deep learning techniques. The impact will extend to researchers and the public, preventing the spread of misinformation, streamlining research processes, and advancing data science through interdisciplinary collaboration.

Example 1: Meta-data Science

In meta data science, we advanced three lines of research this year: (1) the discovery of data dependencies, (2) the design of data spaces, and (3) the management of data integrity.

Our main contribution towards (1) is the first complete approach towards the discovery of meaningful functional dependencies (FDs), published in [1] below, which is made up from the set of true positives arising from the output of algorithms that return all FDs that hold on a given data set and the set of false negatives. The publication does not only advance the state-of-the-art on FD discovery algorithms by a new hybrid approach, but also includes first methods on ranking the outputs to separate true from false positives, and on ranking FDs not included in the output to separate false from true negatives.

In addressing (2), we have published a first approach [3] towards the design of SQL table schemata that is not only based on FDs but also cardinality constraints, which improves state-of-the-art by the ability of quantifying the achievements of classical relational database design by measuring update inefficiency

and join efficiency already during the logical design of SQL table schemata, where no data is present yet. The measure is shown experimentally to be a strong predictor at the operational level, ones data tables have become available. Furthermore, we have parameterized classical relational Boyce-Codd Normal Form (BCNF) by the number of minimal keys [4], making it possible to break ties between different schemata in BCNF. As the number of minimal keys quantifies the trade-off between data integrity and data access variety, we have optimized classical relational database design algorithms towards either update efficiency or query efficiency. This has been validated experimentally on benchmark data.

Finally, we have extended state-of-the-art on data integrity management by automating entity integrity management using key sets rather than single keys [5], introducing uniqueness constraints to object stores [6], and utilizing classical keys, functional and multivalued dependencies in data spaces where the dimensions of data volume, variety, and veracity are accommodated [7].

Example 1: Data Science algorithms for Phylogenetic Research

Over the past 12 months, our collaborative team, including Dr. Jonathan Klawitter (postdoc), A/Prof Alex Gavryushkin, and his Ph.D. student Lars Berling, has initiated a ground-breaking research programme on tractable tree distributions. Example 2: Data Science for Biodiversity

The second impact example is measuring genetic diversity and inferring adaptability over time and space. By measuring how genetic diversity is structured in space, and how this influences the ability of species to adapt to changing selection pressures, including climate change, we can inform conservation management decisions that may mitigate the effects of low genetic diversity for threatened species, and can offer insight into the ongoing threat of invasive species establishing in novel areas. We have identified and summarised common diversity metrics from both the genetic/ genomic and ecological literature and have implemented these in R. We have begun to apply these methods to an exemplar genomic dataset of the taonga bird species, the threatened hihi (Notiomystis cincta). Funding and connections with Genomics Aotearoa* have enabled the generation of a high quality genome assembly and population sequencing data for 250 hihi from an intensively monitored population, which our Data Science methods are beginning to be applied to. Eirian Perkins is leading ongoing methods and workflow development that ensure 'live science', such that new data can be imported as it becomes available. She has also commenced the collation of a list of open access datasets that these new methods can be applied to. A substantial associated R package for implementing these models is now available on Github: https://primer-e.github.io/senlm/

In the past year, our Biological Data Science: Modelling biodiversity sub-theme has made exciting progress on our aims to develop reproducible, open source methods to measure genetic diversity and infer adaptability over time and space. We had a new PhD student, Hui Zhen Tan, join the project in November 2022 and she has already completed analyses of the recombination landscape in hihi (stitchbird; Notiomystis cincta). Recombination is a fundamental evolutionary process that ‘shuffles the deck’ of existing genetic variation, creating new combinations of variants. This has important implications for the adaptive potential of species. With this data, Eirian Perkins has built a population simulation model of the hihi metapopulation. This simulation creates an important test case for how we measure and predict adaptive potential in populations with more or less connectivity to other populations and varying population size over time. The simulations are ground-truthed from a large whole genome resequencing dataset of over 300 birds, plus a hihi genome assembly, funded by Genomics Aotearoa*. The exciting combination of methods from the Data Science project applied to data generated via Genomics Aotearoa demonstrates the active connection between and complementarity of the two projects.

The hihi simulation allows us to test the impact of future management decisions on adaptive potential for this taonga threatened species. Moreover, it also provides an important template for application to any wild species where there is some information about population size and connectivity between them, supporting our overall project aim to measuring how genetic diversity is structured in space, and how this influences the ability of species to adapt to changing selection pressures, including climate change. Both Eirian and Hui Zhen are focused on producing reproducible, reusable code; for Eirian in particular this has

meant developing code that is containerised, and has ensured her workflows are set up such that new data can be imported as it becomes available, i.e. ‘live’.

In terms fo applications, this work provides a novel new tool that enables ecologists to track shifts in species’ latitudinal optima – a tell-tale of climate change at large spatial scales. We’ve created R packages for implementing the non-linear model framework, with inclusion of zero-inflated error distributions (for counts, percentage cover, biomass, traits, etc.) to handle a broad range of large-scale ecological data types. One R package fits flexible non-linear species-environment models (senlm) using a maximum likelihood approach; another R package (more recently) embraces a fully Bayesian approach, which leads the way for hierarchical (multi-layer) modelling of key parameters over multiple species, locations, time-frames, etc.

With postdoc Paulo Martins, we’ve used this modelling framework to analyse the effects of climate change on spatial distributions of hundreds of species of birds in North America. We found that one-third of the 209 species analysed showed significant shifts in optimum latitude over the past 50 years (predominant poleward shifts, particularly in Western regions), and we were able also to use these models to show that the sizes of shifts were linked to key physiological traits, most notably birds’ thermal optima – a clear signal of the effects of climate change.

This research has produced 8 papers and 12 seminars or talks over the reporting period. Importantly, it has also led to the the 'publishing' of related datasets and two open source R packages:

Vision Mātauranga

Research fellow Alistair Lamont has been working closely with Profs Phillip Wilcox and Mik Black to develop a new, efficient, genome simulator that can incorporate key geographic and structural parameters. This is the next step in the design of tools appropriate for genomic analysis and prediction within specific culture communities, particularly iwi not well-served by existing tools with generic assumptions and potential euro-centric data biases. The simulator will also be critical for the design and implementation (in collaboration with Bryant and Gardner) of new analytical tools.

Phil Wilcox has also given several talks across NZ and wider on the important considerations of Precision Medicine, from a Māori Data Sovereignty perspective. His population simulation project as part of this grant complements the research he is doing on the Rakeiora and Variome projects that are funded via Genomics Aoteatoa.
