Public Statement

(300 words)

The team has achieved significant research progress this year in three distinct areas: (i)  phylogenetic analysis—studying the relationships between genomics and disease, (ii) ecological modelling—contrasting geographical and genomic differences within endangered species and (iii) live science publishing—creating a new way of publishing science experiments embedded within traditional research articles.  In each of these areas we have developed new methods and related software that has been shared openly with the science community.

Collaborations within our team are now leading to additional grant proposals in the areas of AI and genomics, and we have strengthened our collaborations with other research partners based in Aotearoa (notably ESR and Genomics Aotearoa).

We sponsored a significant national outreach & training event (ResBaz 2021) enabling hundreds of applied researchers throughout the country to learn new data science skills that they can apply in their own research.  We are also now training a future generation of NZ data scientists via our PhDs and post-doctoral scholarships.  

Taken together, this progress  is producing the new methods, code, collaborations and workforce that will ensure Aotearoa can take full advantage of data science in its future research endeavours in key industries and branches of government.

Live Data Science—closing the gap between doing science and publishing science

We have been working on our Live data Science demonstrators, focussing for now on how to bring publications and report writing more in line with modern eScience toolchains and workflows. Team members at Universities of Auckland and Canterbury have worked together to develop a first prototype of a live science pipeline that fully links live data ingestion through computational workflows to dynamic publication—i.e. a publication that updates itself as the data it consumes is either updated or improved. The prototype pipeline integrates an instrument data collection with a workflow defined in the Common Workflow Language and web-based front-end to model vegetation water-stress levels in real time using satellite data that is regularly updated. This work has been fruitful as it has helped us to develop a metamodel for representing live science experiments that we will be using more broadly going forward. This metamodel will allow us build interchangeable live science publishing tools for scientists working in a wide variety of computational environments. Our first publication on this work has been accepted for publication and presentation at the 18th IEEE International eScience Conference in Salt Lake City, Utah later this year (A. Ellerm, B. Adams, M. Gahegan, and L. Trombach (2022) Enabling LivePublication. In: 2022 IEEE 18th International Conference on eScience).

We can now take what we’ve learned from the prototype development and collaborate with other groups on the grant. Our next step in this regard is to extend this prototype to address the urgent need for live phylogenetic modelling of infectious diseases within New Zealand, including new COVID19 variants. We intend to complete a Case Study for MBIE on this Live Data Science demonstrator over the next few months.

Transparent Data Science—increasing trust in scientific claims

Learning how to verify the claims in scientific papers and “science releases” (media announcements of scientific findings) is a difficult task for both artificial intelligence (AI) systems and humans. However, this task is crucial because learning to separate verified facts from speculation or falsehoods has important consequences. Success at this task can help the reader understand scientific topics and promote science. Conversely, failure at this task leads to the spread of misinformation and exaggeration, which can cause distortion in scientific communication and undermine public confidence in science.

Training machine learning models to successfully perform scientific fact-checking tasks is challenging due to the expertise bottleneck that limits the availability of appropriate training datasets. In this task, models use textual evidence to confirm scientific claims, which requires data that can be used to train the models—i.e extensive domain-expert annotation. Consequently, the number of existing scientific-fact-checking datasets and the sizes of those datasets are limited. However, these limitations do not apply to multiple-choice question datasets because of the necessity of domain exams in the modern education system.

As one of the first steps towards addressing the fact-checking dataset scarcity problem in scientific domains, we have developed a workflow for automatically converting multiple-choice questions into fact-checking data, which we call Multi2Claim. By applying the proposed workflow, we generated two large-scale datasets for scientific-fact-checking tasks: Med-Fact and Gsci-Fact for the medical and general science domains, respectively. These two datasets are among the first examples of large-scale scientific-fact-checking datasets and as such are useful to many researchers—and are being shared. We developed baseline models for the verdict prediction task using each dataset. Additionally, we demonstrated that the datasets could be used to improve performance with respect to the F 1 weighted metric on existing fact-checking datasets such as SciFact, HEALTHVER, COVID-Fact, and CLIMATE-FEVER. In some cases, the improvement in performance was up to a 26\% increase.

Another challenge that makes applying cutting-edge deep learning models (transformer-based models) to scientific data difficult (scientific textual data and genomic sequences) is the models' input length limitation. Where the data source is long, current models cannot scale to the subtle complexities and relationships that they contain.

In this regard, we have made significant progress in the following two areas. (1) We were able to apply a linearized version of the transformer-based models to genome-wide association studies (GWAS) data. By using a scalable (long input) on the linearized version of the transformer-based models and appropriately encoding Single Nucleotide Variants (SNVs), we were able to achieve competitive initial performance for prediction of gout disease (with an AUROC of 83% when classifying a balanced test set using genotype and demographic information). (Outcomes of collaboration with Canterbury University on the GWAS project). (2) We also investigated whether the internal mechanisms (attention mechanism) of off-the shelf models (BERT, GPT-2) can be practically used to overcome the input length limitation. To this end, we used layer-based attention scores to shorten input length. We examined our methodology both in the general domain text corpus (IMDB dataset) and the scientific papers corpus (scholarly papers in four distinct fields: computer science, mathematics, biology, and physics). We showed that attention scores could be used to shorten the input length for some downstream tasks, such as topic classification of scientific papers without adversely affecting the quality of the classification tasks, thus allowing us to analyse much longer documents effectively.

Biological and Ecological applications of Data Science—measuring genetic diversity of threatened species

In the past year, our Biological Data Science: Modelling biodiversity sub-theme has continued to develop reproducible, open source methods to measure genetic diversity and infer adaptability over time and space. By measuring how genetic diversity is structured in space, and how this influences the ability of species to adapt to changing selection pressures, including climate change, we can inform conservation management decisions that may mitigate the effects of low genetic diversity for threatened species, and can offer insight into the ongoing threat of invasive species establishing in novel areas. We have identified and summarised common diversity metrics from both the genetic/ genomic and ecological literature and have implemented these in R. We have begun to apply these methods to an exemplar genomic dataset of the taonga bird species, the threatened hihi (Notiomystis cincta). Funding and collaboration with Genomics Aotearoa have enabled the generation of a high quality genome assembly and population sequencing data for 250 hihi from an intensively monitored population, which our Data Science methods are beginning to be applied to. Our current Data Science PhD student (Eirian Perkins) is leading ongoing methods and workflow development that will integrate these methods into our 'live science' workflows, such that new data is automatically imported as it becomes available and the analysis re-run. We are also collating of a list of open access datasets (and associated ecological research questions) that our new methods can be applied to.

Meta-data science

Meta-data science is the (usually automated) discovery of metadata and various schema and semantic information from streams of raw data so the data can be better understood and reused, even if it was not originally well documented when it was produced. This helps increase the reusability of data, a key goal of open science.

The efficiency of database schema that are created by automated means (or even by manual means) can have a dramatic effect on performance. And in any data-intensive environment, performance inefficiency can be crippling. We studied the problem of computing all cross joins that hold on a given data table. Cross joins form the most expressive and costly query operation, and the degree by which cross joins hold provide us also with a measure how independent feature sets are in a given data set. This leads to effective query optimization techniques.

One new outcome is the creation of a new normal form (normal form is a term used to describe the logical consistency of a database schema) for relational schema design that quantifies the update inefficiency and join efficiency at schema design time, and additionally an algorithm that computes a schema in that normal form with a minimal level of update inefficiency achievable. This new normal form improves limitations of classical schema design since it takes into account new meta data in the form of cardinality constraints, while classical design is based on functional dependencies only.

The properties of the schema translate into the performance of physical database operations such as updates and joins, so they have a substantial, and measurable positive impact on performance of queries/transactions involving large datasets. This new method offers quantifiable benefits to the design of any relational database, thus has very wide applicability beyond data science.

LinguaPhylo—a domain-specific modelling environment for phylogenetics research

The first impact example is LinguaPhylo (https://linguaphylo.github.io).  LinguaPhylo (or LPhy for short) is a new domain-specific modeling language with supporting open source software for visualisation, simulation and inference. LPhy fills a crucial gap in the ecosystem for computational biology tools focused on probabilistic modelling of molecular evolution, phylogenetics and genealogical inference. There has been impressive progress in the sophistication of models implemented and applied to comparative molecular data sets, but alongside this progress has been a lack of tools that assist in pedagogy, reproducibility and reusability of such models. Specifically there has been a lack of development of high-level formal languages for compact, precise model specification that are both human-friendly and computer-readable. In this work we address that gap by introducing a high-level extensible declarative specification language with implicit typing and array programming features that allows compact and precise descriptions of cutting-edge probabilistic models for phylogenetic analyses that are both human- and computer-readable. We have implemented a bridge between this new language and the popular Bayesian inference tool BEAST 2, and we have also developed the LPhy Studio software package for:

  1. graphical visualisation—so that the modelling workflow can be more easily understood and shared

  2. narrative generation—such that a text description of any model can be automatically produced, again to improve understandability & transparency and

  3. simulation of models specified in the new LinguaPhylo language.

The first paper on this work is available as a preprint and is in review in PLoS Computational Biology.

Stencila—Open-source desktop science publishing and sharing

The second impact example is Stencila, our industry partner. Over the past year Stencila has been focussed on development of version 2.0 of our platform for publishing reproducible research. We have taken feedback from users and re-architected the platform to be easier to install, easier to use and more integrated with other formats and platforms. We are currently wrapping this development up, moving towards writing documentation and a launch of version 2.0 in October 2022. Key features include:

1. Integrations: Bidirectional conversion to/from the following document formats is supported: Markdown, Jupyter Notebooks, R Markdown, Microsoft Word, Google Docs, HTML, JSON, JSON5, YAML, TOML

We have also developed technology to incorporate executable elements such as code chunks into Microsoft Word and Google Docs, formats that normally do not support such elements. This allows data scientists to more easily collaborate with others by being able to create and share "executable DOCX" files that can have changes made to their content and then converted back to executable formats such as Jupyter. In other words, it combines the positive benefits of open research tools and tools to report about such research.

Importantly, this closes the gap between the ‘doing’ science and the ‘reporting’ science, a gap that currently leads to inconsistencies and inaccuracies, and is in large part responsible for the reproducibility crisis in science.

2. Reactivity—live science within a desktop environment
A major part of our version 2 development has been making Stencila documents fully reactive (to changes in underpinning data—see the first section of Strategic Intent above) . This allows any changes made within the document itself, or to external files, to be reflected in the outputs of the document. To this end we have implemented document dependency analysis. For each document we create a dependency graph, based on semantic analysis of code, which includes elements within the document (e.g. Parameters, CodeChunks) and other files (e.g. a CSV data file). Using this dependency graph, we are able to make the document reactive to changes. For example, if a code chunk reads a CSV file and the integrated file watcher sees that the CSV file has changed, the code chunk will be re-executed.

3. Reproducibility
We have made it easier to create lightweight reproducible compute environments for documents. We have built on top of tools such as ASDF (https://asdf-vm.com/), modern package managers such as Poetry (https://python-poetry.org/) and Renv (https://rstudio.github.io/renv/), and the Taskfile format (https://taskfile.dev/) to reduce the friction for users in going from their local development environment to being able to publish reproducible research directly on the web.

As far as code outputs, there were 45 releases of Stencila (ignoring patch releases) in the past year (0.113-0.142 and 1.0 to 1.16; https://github.com/stencila/stencila/releases), which gives some indication of the dramatic pace of progress with this project.

Vision Mātauranga *

We continue to teach the principles around Māori Data Sovereignty (MDS) in all of our Research Data Workshops and including at the ResBaz event described above (investing in people section). Many researchers warm to the values at the heart of MDS once they understand the reasonable motivations behind them. Practically, this means that hundreds of researchers have now heard a simple introduction to MDS and have been exposed to its core values.

The genome-wide association studies mentioned under Domestic Collaboration are specifically focussed on people with Māori and Pacifica ancestry, to try to better understand the genetic factors in this disease so that there might be better health outcomes for those affected individuals. Specifically, genetic analysis of health conditions in humans is most often done using GWAS (genome-wide association studies) in large studies (n > 105 participants) genotyped via genome-wide DNA marker panels and/or DNA sequencing-based techniques. Not only does this allow specific genomic regions associated with conditions to be identified, it also enables genome-wide estimation of both heritability and disease risk/resilience (DR). Study designs and predictive methodologies which do not consider these genetic relationships require somewhat larger datasets to effectively detect sufficient genetic regions for PRS-based research and/or clinical applications (ref). However, such datasets do not generally include many indigenous peoples, who have important genetic differences from more commonly studied populations of typically European descent. Moreover, existing datasets from Māori (and Pasifika) domiciled in New Zealand are few, and those that could be utilised, consist of fewer than two thousand individuals – typically from case control studies (reference) – so are underpowered for clinically accurate DR prediction. In addition, establishing sufficiently powered GWAS is highly unlikely in Aotearoa/NZ because of substantive costs associated with generating genotypic data. Māori communities have also been historically reluctant to engage in medical genomics research, thus any studies need to ensure clinically implementable estimates of DR can be obtained to justify participation of such communities.

Our approach combines statistical simulation, and new prediction methodologies, and incorporation of Māori knowledge regarding whakapapa, to collectively determine optimal study and analysis strategies for DR prediction in Te Ao Māori.

Bayesian phylodynamic inference from single-cell sequencing data

===================================================

The second project carries on from recent work by Kylie Chen on developing Bayesian evolutionary inference tools for the study of single-cell sequencing data, especially for studying cancer evolution in individual patients. Single-cell sequencing provides a new way to explore the evolutionary history of cell lineages in vivo. Compared to traditional bulk sequencing, where a population of heterogeneous cells is pooled to form a single observation, single-cell sequencing isolates and amplifies genetic material from individual cells, thereby preserving the information about the origin of the sequences. However, single-cell data are more error-prone than bulk sequencing data due to the limited genomic material available per cell. We have developed computational models to account for error and mutation in such data and allow for evolutionary inference of single-cell data within a mature and extensible open source Bayesian framework, BEAST2. This first piece of work has recently been published in Molecular Biology and Evolution. Following on from this we have submitted a Marsden grant (currently in the second round) to substantially expand our collaboration on this work to include international colleagues in ETH and University of Vigo.

  1. Discovery of meta data from data:

    • We studied the problem of computing all cross joins that hold on a given data table. Cross joins form the most expressive and costly query operation, and the degree by which cross joins hold provide us also with a measure how independent feature sets are in a given data set. This leads to effective query optimization techniques. We showed that the decision variant of the problem is not only NP-complete but even W[3]-complete in the size of the output. Despite of these efficiency barriers, we developed several algorithms that work efficiently within those limits. We also extended the algorithms to compute the degree by which cross joins hold. 

  2. Application of meta data to data preparation, in particular the design of data models with respect to different data quality dimensions. 

    • One outcome is the proposal of a new normal form for relational schema design that quantifies the update inefficiency and join efficiency at schema design time, and an algorithm that computes a schema in that normal form with a minimal level of update inefficiency achievable. The properties of the schema translate into the performance of physical database operations such as updates and joins. The new normal form improves limitations of classical schema design since it takes into account new meta data in the form of cardinality constraints, while classical design is based on functional dependencies only.

    • A second outcome includes a refinement of the classical Boyce-Codd Normal Form by parameterizing the number of minimal keys exhibited as a measure of both access variety and update complexity. We showed - whenever possible - how to compute a dependency-preserving Boyce-Codd Normal Form decomposition that exhibits a globally minimum number of minimal keys, thereby optimizing update complexity. If a dependency-preserving Boyce-Codd Normal Form decomposition does not exist, our algorithm still returns a schema in Third Normal Form that minimizes the sub-schemata that are not in Boyce-Codd Normal Form, and minimizes also the number of minimal keys exhibited by the sub-schemata that are in Boyce-Codd Normal Form. Again, the properties of the schemata translate into the performance of physical database operations such as updates and queries.

Over the past year Stencila has been focussed on development of version 2.0 of our platform for publishing reproducible research. We have taken feedback from users and re-architected the platform to be easier to install, easier to use and more integrated with other formats and platforms. We are currently wrapping this development up, moving towards writing documentation and a launch of version 2.0 in October. Key features include:

# Integrations

Stencila aims to be compatible with many formats to meet the varied preferences of users (both for formats and software). Bidirectional conversion to/from the following document formats is supported:

- Markdown
- Jupyter Notebooks
- R Markdown
- Microsoft Word
- Google Docs
- HTML
- JSON
- JSON5
- YAML
- TOML

In addition, we have converters in progress for LaTeX and OrgMode. We support lossless, or close to lossless conversion for all formats. For example, we have developed technology to incorporate executable elements such as code chunks into Microsoft Word and Google Docs -
formats that normally do not support such elements. This allows data scientists to more easily collaborate with others by being able to send the "executable DOCX" files that can have changes made to them and then converted back to executable formats such as Jupyter
Notebooks.

# Reactivity

A major part of our version 2 development has been making Stencila documents fully reactive. This allows changes made within the document itself, or to external files, to be reflected in the outputs of the document. To this end we have implemented document dependency analysis. For each document we create a dependency graph, based on semantic analysis of code, which includes elements within the document (e.g. Parameters, CodeChunks) and other files (e.g. a CSV data file). Using this dependency graph, we are able to make the document reactive to changes. For example, if a code chunk reads a CSV file and the integrated file watcher sees that the CSV file has changed, the code chunk will be re-executed.

# Reproducibility

We have made it easier to create lightweight reproducible compute environments for documents. We have built on top of tools such as ASDF (https://asdf-vm.com/), modern package managers such as Poetry (https://python-poetry.org/) and Renv (https://rstudio.github.io/renv/), and the Taskfile format (https://taskfile.dev/) to reduce the friction for users in going from their local development environment to being able to publish reproducible research on the web.

Learning how to verify the claims in scientific papers and “science releases” (media announcements of scientific findings) is a difficult task for both artificial intelligence (AI) systems and humans. However, this task is crucial because learning to separate verified facts from speculation or falsehoods has important consequences. Success at this task can help the reader understand scientific topics and promote science. Conversely, failure at this task leads to the spread of misinformation and exaggeration, which can cause distortion in scientific communication and undermine public confidence in science.

Training machine learning models to successfully perform scientific fact-checking tasks is challenging due to the expertise bottleneck that limits the availability of appropriate training datasets. In this task, models use textual evidence to confirm scientific claims, which requires data that contains extensive domain-expert annotation. Consequently, the number of existing scientific-fact-checking datasets and the sizes of those datasets are limited. However, these limitations do not apply to multiple-choice question datasets because of the necessity of domain exams in the modern education system.

As one of the first steps towards addressing the fact-checking dataset scarcity problem in scientific domains, we have developed a workflow for automatically converting multiple-choice questions into fact-checking data, which we call Multi2Claim. By applying the proposed workflow, we generated two large-scale datasets for scientific-fact-checking tasks: Med-Fact and Gsci-Fact for the medical and general science domains, respectively. These two datasets are among the first examples of large-scale scientific-fact-checking datasets and as such are useful to many researchers—and are being shared. We developed baseline models for the verdict prediction task using each dataset. Additionally, we demonstrated that the datasets could be used to improve performance with respect to the F 1 weighted metric on existing fact-checking datasets such as SciFact, HEALTHVER, COVID-Fact, and CLIMATE-FEVER. In some cases, the improvement in performance was up to a 26\% increase.

Another challenge that makes applying cutting-edge deep learning models (transformer-based models) to scientific data difficult (scientific textual data and genomic sequences) is the models' input length limitation. Where the data source is long, current models cannot scale to the subtle complexities and relationships that they contain.

In this regard, we summarised the achieved results as follows:

We were able to apply a linearized version of the transformer-based models to genome-wide association studies (GWAS) data. By using a scalable (long input) on the linearized version of the transformer-based models and appropriately encoding Single Nucleotide Variants (SNVs), we were able to achieve competitive initial performance for prediction of gout disease (with an AUROC of 83% when classifying a balanced test set using genotype and demographic information). (Outcomes of collaboration with Canterbury University on the GWAS project)

We also investigated whether the internal mechanisms (attention mechanism) of off-the shelf models (BERT, GPT-2) can be practically used to overcome the input length limitation. To this end, we used layer-based attention scores to shorten input length. We examined our methodology both in the general domain text corpus (IMDB dataset) and the scientific papers corpus (scholarly papers in four distinct fields: computer science, mathematics, biology, and physics). We showed that attention scores could be used to shorten the input length for some downstream tasks, such as topic classification of scientific papers. (Outcomes of Neset TAN’s (PhD student) preliminary research questions)

Automatically deriving text from data and code is a major focus of our work in three areas.

  3. Our research on scientific fact-checking is targetted at inconsistencies between the text descriptions and trusted data, code, or other texts.
