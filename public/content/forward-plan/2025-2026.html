Beyond Prediction: Live and Transparent Data Science

Our strategic direction focuses on developing new data science methods that actually improve the conduct of research, and to demonstrate some of these improvements in the life science, specifically: genomics, population health and ecology. We provide a high-level summary of our work towards these strategic directions below.

Our Live Data Science initiative aims at closing the gap between the ‘doing’ of science and the ‘publishing’ of science, and in doing so also make it easier and more transparent to report and share research findings. In an era when data are ubiquitous and regularly updated, the lag in writing and publishing science—whether in academic journals or in reports for the government—becomes restrictive and is a source of great inefficiency.

Our Transparent Data Science initiative concentrates on the challenge of improving the trustworthiness and reproducibility of data-intensive research, providing tools to question scientific claims and query research processes. This opens up research to more scrutiny, which helps to improve trust, and offers new avenues for public engagement.

Our Applications of Data Science in the Life Sciences tackles a multitude of longstanding analytical challenges and problems in genomics, ecology and population health, including: (i) new techniques to improve the accuracy of phylodynamic modeling, with applications into infectious diseases, (ii) modelling the entire genone of a species and using this to anticipate adaptation capacity to a changing environment (iii) understanding the complex relationships between population genomics and common illnesses, to better target health interventions and (iv) better understanding and thus ability to control the spread of infectious diseases through susceptible populations.

The extremely rapid advancement of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) has created many new opportunities in science, both in the sense of improving productivity with more generic (desktop) AI assistants, and providing entirely new ways to solve very complex science questions. This re-orientation of science and AI is a major driver of some of our activities for 2025-6, as shown in the sections below.

Our overall focus remains on translating progress in data science and AI into new tools and workflows that can provide advantage in areas that matter for New Zealand, as we balance our support for primary industries with our need to provide a sustainable and healthy ecosystem to support them. The report below describes our planned work in genomics, biological science, population health and ecology, empowered by our research into explainable and transparent data science.

Strategic intent

In the coming year, our Explainable Data Science team will be conducting a mechanistic analysis of LLM model distillation. Knowledge distillation compresses large language models (LLMs) into smaller, more efficient versions by training a student model with fewer parameters to mimic the outputs of a larger teacher model, while maintaining comparable performance. This leads to benefits in terms of computation, memory, and speed leading to wider adoption and utility of models, meaning that the same model can be run reliably on a much smaller computational platform. This implies bigger impacts for New Zealand for high-stakes applications, such as LLMs for genomics, materials science, climate science and drug discovery that can be run locally, on modest computational infrastructure. However, the underlying transformations that occur remain poorly understood. The focus in this year will be on the impact on a model's internal circuits, representations spaces, and activation patterns.

The research questions we will be pursuing include:

  • How significantly does the distillation process alter the underlying internal representations and learned algorithms?

  • What effect does distillation have on a model's robustness?

  • How can an alignment metric best quantify structural and functional differences between a teacher and a student model?

By providing a mechanistic understanding of the changes that take place during knowledge distillation, we aim to improve trust in LLMs used for science. It will also help in designing more effective student models and mitigating potential failure cases.

The 2025-2026 phase of our LivePublication project (see previous reports) applies the now matured workflow to case study applications, engaging in cross-disciplinary research opportunities. LivePublication bridges the gap between computational research and scholarly communication through FAIR-compliant, automated, and reproducible publication systems for entire scientific experiments. A major goal of the work for the coming year is piloting LivePublication implementations with local researchers and institutions. Potential impacts here are large, such as improving the effectiveness and reliability of science, and ensuring that all the relevant details of computationally-led research are recorded, communicated and preserved.

In the coming year for our biodiversity and ecology team, research fellow William Pearman will complete a second, significant piece of work using simulations to assess the ability of the microbiome - the resident microbes in and on a host - to buffer environmental changes experience by the host. William's work in the past year has illustrated that for hosts with limited genetic diversity the microbiome is an important buffer that enables hosts to persist in otherwise unsuitable environments, but that there is some potential for 'conflict' – that which is good for the microbes to persist is not necessarily good for the host persistence. In the coming year we will build further complexity into this model to assess both cooperation and conflict for hosts with more genetic diversity - when, as a host, does it make sense to rely more on your microbiome, and can this reliance be reversed if environmental conditions become more benign? 

Our Genomics Data Science continue work on improving the data analytics of genomics data. Particularly focussing on elements involving distinguishing genuine and useful biological signals and from biological and experimental noise. Better understanding of the errors and uncertainties of genomics data is of strategic importance to many industries in New Zealand, as we work to maximise the effectiveness and minimise the side-effects of commercial agriculture.

Our research will focus on advancing Bayesian phylogenetic methods through three complementary projects in the coming year. The first project will evaluate Bayesian inference performance on single-cell DNA sequencing tumour data using BEAST2, addressing the lack of realistic simulation tools in this field. The second will implement calibrated coalescent point process (CPP) models within LinguaPhylo to support well-calibrated evolutionary studies. The third will develop a comprehensive Bayesian phylogenetic model averaging framework using Reversible Jump Markov Chain Monte Carlo models (RJMCMC) to account for model uncertainty rather than relying on single models. Together, these projects aim to significantly improve the accuracy, reproducibility, and computational efficiency of evolutionary analyses in biological research.

Our work in the biodiversity and ecology space will leverage both empirical and simulated data to develop new methods and approaches that enable us to understand the ability of species to adapt to their rapidly changing environment. These tools and approaches therefore have direct application to our ability to assess both the ability of threatened species to adapt and potentially 'rebound' from the brink of extinction, and the potential of invasive species to continue to expand their range, and hence economic, ecosystem and health impacts that are key to both sustainability and agricultural goals. 

Our Genomics Data Science will deliver substantial scientific benefits through multiple pathways. The simulation study will enhance evolutionary reconstruction accuracy in cancer research by providing a realistic pipeline for evaluating variant callers and inference models. The calibrated coalescent point process (CPP) implementation will improve evolutionary timescale estimation by allowing researchers to control important calibration points. The RJMCMC (Reversible Jump Markov Chain Monte Carlo) framework will enable more reliable phylogenetic trees through model averaging. We anticipate multiple high-impact publications and the release of open-source software tools that will benefit fields including conservation genetics, epidemiology, and cancer evolution, and an commensurate improvement in the reliability of predictive models in genomics.

Excellence (Science excellence)

Three high impact research projects are under development with our Genomics Data Science team. In brief, one explores the evidence supporting functional human genes, a second deploys a model based on this evidence to confirm and identify novel functional human genes. A third project will assess the impact of RNA structure on RNA turnover, this is a topical area due to the development of computational tools for RNA vaccine design. All projects will target high-impact venues in natural language processing and AI (e.g., ACL, ACM, EMNLP). Each integrates state-of-the-art techniques, rigorous empirical evaluation, and interdisciplinary relevance.

Our projects represent cutting-edge research in computational biology. The calibrated CPP development provides a general solution to a long-standing problem, significantly expanding the application of accurate calibration to birth-death models in phylogenetics. The RJMCMC project pioneers a comprehensive model-averaging approach validated through extensive simulations and case studies. All projects aim for publication in high-impact journals in evolutionary biology and computational biology, with presentations at international conferences including SMBE 2025 Beijing (July 20-24) planned to demonstrate scientific excellence and gather expert feedback.

Our Data Science in Biodiversity & Ecology team will continue to build on our innovative approaches to measure genetic diversity and infer adaptability over time and space, while ensuring our work is open source and reproducible. In the past year, we have generated whole genome sequences for a further 200 hihi (stitchbird) individuals, taking our total number sequenced to over 450 individuals of this threatened, taonga species. We have utilised highly accurate genome sequences from a reference set of 30 individuals to 'fill the gaps' of missing genetic data from the lower-cost sequencing of the 450 individuals. This has enabled genome-wide diversity estimates and revealed considerable variation between individuals in their genetic diversity. Crucially, with this data now in hand, in the coming year we will be linking this genetic diversity data to individual fitness (measured as the number of offspring a bird has over its lifetime), with the hypothesis that genetically diverse individuals are more resilient, live longer and have more opportunities for reproduction than low diversity individuals. This will be one of the most comprehensive assessments ever of the relationship between genetic diversity and individual health for a threatened species. Our code and workflow will be available on github and available to others in the community to complete a genetic health assessment of other threatened species.

In Transparent Data Science we will publish peer-reviewed papers on the LivePublication platform, highlighting provenance modelling and implementation strategies. LivePublication pilots will be hosted on open platforms to provide value in their format. There are ongoing conversations regarding how these publications interface with traditional publication venues, and some interest from more traditional publishers in experimenting with our new approach.  Our work is contributing to the development of international standards in three important areas of open science: (i) scientific workflows, (ii) representing provenance and (iii) containerisation of research objects (RO-Crate), demonstrating value within the international research community. 

With our commercial partner, Stencila, we will also be working on richer representations of research experiments and their translation into research articles. The approach we shall be pioneering over the coming year uses domain-specific Multimodel GraphRAG databases in this regard to improve the accuracy of AI-based methods to help generate, or evaluate, research products such as reports, computer programs, scripts and workflows. GraphRAG is a new field where knowledge graphs are used to help with AI Retrieval Augmented Generation; the GraphRAG process involves creating a knowledge graph out of raw text, or other information sources then building a graph of community knowledge, generating summaries for a community, and then leveraging these structures when perform RAG-based tasks, such as automated text or code generation. Using our existing document converters were are able to chunk documents into structurally and semantically meaningful document "nodes" (e.g. paragraphs, table and figure captions, images, equations), not just chunks of text as in naive RAG. We canonicalize identifiers within documents using the OpenAlex API: DOIs for articles and the references they cite, ORCIDs for researchers, and ROR for research organizations. In addition, we use LLMs to perform named entity recognition on the content of a document (e.g. identify mention of gene or proteins within a table caption. These document nodes are stored in a graph database which allows precise retrieval of context based on the user's query and/or the content of the document that they are currently authoring. For example, retrieving all tables where a specific gene was mentioned in the caption in articles written after a specific date.

We will be embedding models specific to particular domains (e.g. PubMedBERT) to also add vector search capabilities to the graph database. We are developing a simple language, DocsSQL to make querying of these databases more intuitive, and less error prone for both humans and AI agentic systems.
